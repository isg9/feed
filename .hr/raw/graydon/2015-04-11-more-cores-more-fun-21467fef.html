For what it's worth, Aaron Turon's recent post Fearless Concurrency explains, better than I've ever managed to, the interplay of affine types, static lifetimes, static control of aliasing and mutability in Rust, and the resulting safe-concurrency superpowers users are granted, for putting up with all the strict checking.

I recommend it for anyone curious why they'd bother with "yet another language", or especially anyone who's found themselves surprised / annoyed when wrestling with the type system and borrow checker: it's enforcing -- and providing -- much stronger guarantees than most languages are doing.

(P.S. If that doesn't convince you, perhaps someone equally lucid will write up all the self-pointer and iterator invalidations that other languages tolerate, that Rust prevents! But I think the concurrency results are a little more striking to newcomers.)

(P.P.S. The only thing I'd quibble with in Aaron's post is the very early statement, "Initially these problems seemed orthogonal, but to our amazement, the solution turned out to be identical"; I assume he's writing this as a rhetorical device to engage the reader, because there was no such amazement involved, aside perhaps from being amazed that things we had merely theoretical faith in would ultimately work out -- eventually, after many attempts -- so well in practice. If there's been any guiding principle of the rust project from the beginning, it's been that faith that correct concurrency and general memory safety are two sides of the same coin, that both benefit from similar techniques of isolation and mutability control.

Right back in the first published design docs we have paragraphs like this one or quotes from other designers explaining our influences like these. It's just that we had to try about nine different implementation variants to get the rules around aliasing, ownership and mutability to all line up nicely enough to meet our goals. But I'll happily forgive the flourish, it's such a great post :)
