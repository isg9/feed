I'm hoping to do a little more reading in 2024 than I managed in 2023 (at least in part due to work calming down a bit). This week I finished off two books from last year.


The Free World: Art and Thought in the Cold War

This was a bit of a slog but in a good way. It's an exploration of -- to paraphrase the author -- what people were thinking in the west, during the middle of the 20th century, mainly from the 40s through late 60s. The millieu in which the parents of Boomers were immersed, and that Boomers themselves overheard as children. What subjects preoccupied people living through time time. What their intellectual, philosophical, political, artistic, and cultural frames of reference were. It covers a fairly wide range of subjects (and a decently wide though incomplete set of specific groups), through political arguments, philosophical and critical writing, painting, art criticism, poetry and novels, writing about colonialism and race, about gender and sexuality, music, theatre, film-making, popular culture, etc. etc.

The author approaches his task with genuine interest and curiosity, as well as commendable depth and breadth. He knows his material. But he's not trying to hammer you with a specific reading. He's trying to get inside the heads of the culture of the time, specifically (though not too narrowly) with respect to a central theme in the writing and thinking of that generation: the idea and experience of freedom. As an existential-philosophical or psychological state; as a set of movements within a generation of artists and thinkers; as a set of cultural events and self-perceptions.

He does a good job at this, mixing ample primary material with his own sharp critical analysis: sometimes accepting and reflecting what people said or did, other times pointing out how they were bullshitting or overplaying their hands, always putting people and ideas in context of their social scene, intellectual influences, economic condition, critical reception. I got a much better feel for the 20th century's cultural movements as a set of conversations than I'd had before, which I really value.

It's structured almost as a set of separate essays on groups, people or movements, though often there's some connection or reflection back to an earlier group. It's not temporally linear, but does roughly move from earlier-to-later as it proceeds.

It's also quite funny at times. Each now and then he inserts a little jab at someone's (or some group's) hypocrisy, arrogance or self-indulgence in a way that, while vaguely sympathetic, is also hilariously deadly.

Datapoint: The Lost Story of the Texans Who Invented the Personal Computer Revolution

Datapoint is not a name most people will recognize anymore, though for as much as a decade it was a major player in the period of transition between institutional and personal computing: roughly the 1970s, though they were active in the 60s through 80s or even, in residual form, the 90s. Datapoint's claim to fame is that it was (a) first or nearly-first over the line of a "personal computer" (a single user terminal with enough autonomy to be usable on its own, disconnected from its host), and (b) that the CPU design, which it contracted to Intel for manufacturing, was roughly speaking the ancestral design of the x86. So if you squint you can convince yourself that today's x86 PC architecture starts with the team at Datapoint, in Texas, in 1969-70. That argument is mostly what this book is trying to do.

The book is .. ok. Not thrilling. It does what it sets out to do, but not with any real dazzle or even much in the way of memorable anecdotes. The most exciting part is the early product development phase in the first third of the book (also the only part with much technical material, and even that is pretty light). Datapoint was successful only for a few years, after which it suffered a mix of technical, interpersonal, and especially financial and organizational crises, including being taken over by a corporate raider in the early 80s recession, and it really never recovered. Like many firms it existed in a zombie form for many years thereafter, gradually being dismembered as its service contracts and comeback attempts all fizzled out.

To me the most memorable and telling part is where the book describes "the first known employment, by end users, of what is now called a personal computer" at a chicken farm in El Dorado, Arkansas, allowing the Pillsbury company to run a payroll program overnight, print local cheques and transmit results back to Pillsbury HQ in Minneapolis. I kinda wish I was on the board of the Computer History Museum so I could argue for putting in a little mock-up exhibit of a Datapoint 2200 connected to a phone line and covered in feathers and chicken guts. Feels like an apt image for the message that computers are capitalism.

Anyway, I picked this book up because I've been a little interested lately in the slightly-off-the-mainstream paths of US computer history: things taking place in both the midwest (CDC/Cray, NCR, Burroughs, Honeywell, Motorola) and in Texas (TI, Datapoint, Cirrus Logic, National Instruments, Compaq, Dell). In contrast to the mainstream paths of US computer history that focus on Silicon Valley (Fairchild, NatSemi, Intel, AMD, HP, Sun and SGI; Stanford, SRI, PARC) and the earlier companies in the northeast (IBM, Sperry, DEC, Data General, Apollo, Bell Labs, UNIVAC). It's helping me put pieces together in terms of the relationship between the computer business and older industries, especially military contractors (Datapoint was all former General Dynamics guys and TRW did all the overseas business) as well as finance (its fate definitely hinged on wall street's favor). Once the corporate raider took over it was all down hill.

The only other interesting bit is the (very briefly told) history of ARCNET -- also a Datapoint product -- which lost to Ethernet, but not as badly as Datapoint machines in general lost to IBM PCs. Datapoint has a compelling claim here to having invented LANs, or at least being first to market with them (Ethernet existed but only in a lab). So that's a thing! Linux still supports ARCNET and I remember at least periodically encountering it in real deployments in the 90s. Apparently you can still buy equipment for it for certain use cases? Miracles never cease.
