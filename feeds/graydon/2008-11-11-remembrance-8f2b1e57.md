# remembrance

As I pass into another year's remembrance day, I'm coincidentally reading a section of Studs Terkel's The Good War, which is a collection of oral histories of WWII survivors (vets and non), as well as listening to the segment of Berkeley History 7B on WWI, which is now about the 6th time I've covered the period in various courses. Both face the day's rhetoric in rather stark contrast.

I'm more annoyed this year than ever before to see the press roll out the same stupid, ignorant words as always: "our glorious dead", "sad but proud", "honour", "bravery", "sacrifice". These words lay the groundwork for future wars, and should be resisted.

![](http://venge.net/graydon/brute.jpg)

The correct word, the only word we should ever use on a day like this, is "shame". Shame at having been drawn into such a thing, and shame at how it played out. Does anyone "celebrating" remembrance day even *know why* it started? It was the product of a stupid naval arms race, an even stupider and more horrible colonial competition, general social warmongering and incompetent political maneuvering. There wasn't a dash of honour; people cheered it's opening in every country, like idiots. Did America The Good enter for a good reason? No. It sleepwalked into it near the end, after electing a leader promising to stay out, its ruling class sweet-talked by their own [curiously familiar arms dealers](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_P._Bush#Political_prominence), its population buried in crude propaganda. Was the fighting honourable? No, it was a goddamn blood bath. People were gassed, shelled until utterly mad and buried alive in mud. A pandemic of flu broke out. Industrial weapons *nobody had ever faced before* were turned on millions of poor, fresh-faced 18 year old conscripts who hadn't a clue why they were there, mowing them down like grass.

The correct word is shame. So long as we remember "honour", we learn nothing.
