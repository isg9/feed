# the delete key is my favourite

In this culture, we assign a lot of credit to acts of creation, development, growth, production, making and connecting. We assign much less value to the acts of elimination, simplification, reduction, destruction, editing or pruning.

I think this is an incorrect assignment of value. There is great value in the careful, deliberate selection of things to remove, to eliminate, to reduce, to destroy. I was dwelling on this recently, after re-reading an old passage in which Huxley quotes C.D Broad:

> "... the function of the brain and nervous system and sense organs is in the main eliminative and not productive. ... The function of the brain and nervous system is to protect us from being overwhelmed and confused by this mass of largely useless and irrelevant knowledge, ... leaving only that very small and special selection which is likely to be practically useful.'"

(Of course, I edited this down *myself*)

It seems important to me. Many people reading this passage focused on the parts I edited out, imagining the limitless possibilities of Mind at Large, so tempting to the religiously inclined. I'm familiar with, and value, that part of the experience of existing; but I think the interesting part of the quote is that it draws our attention to a source of value: *organizing* and *making practical* through *reduction*. This is a very general principle.

It's the principle that drives science. Science compresses data, eliminates redundancy and noise, filters and edits, until it's left with simple things: linear relations, recurrences, formulae, maps, diagrams, small tight patterns that generate a *lot* of practical utility. It's not the utility that's important; it's the *ratio* between the very-small theory and the very-large utility.

It's the principle that drives many, perhaps most improvements in processes, organization of space and expenditure of energy. And time! Without elimination of redundancy, noise, clutter and irrelevance, nothing ever gets done. We all rely on this to communicate, plan, or act at all.

Reduction and elimination of signal is, I think, a way of *defining* thought, a thinking process. When someone speaks of *abstraction*, or of an idea *in abstract*, they mean a summary. A reduction from the full thing. A compressed version. A brief. Just the highlights. Cut to the chase. Eliminate the details. Even the word derives from the latin, *abstrahere*, drawn-off from the world. Removed from a space.
