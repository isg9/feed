# That&#8217;s a Big Microscope&#8230;

I’ve often said that there are no secrets in hardware — you just need a bigger, better microscope.

I think I’ve found the limit to that statement. To give you an idea, here’s the “lightbulb” that powers the microscope:

![](https://bunniefoo.com/ntw/sls_areal.png)

It’s the size of a building, and it’s the [Swiss Light Source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Light_Source). Actually, not all of that building is dedicated to this microscope, just [one beamline](https://www.psi.ch/sls/csaxs/csaxs) of an X-ray synchrotron capable of producing photons at an energy of 6.5keV (X-rays) at a flux of close to a billion coherent photons per second — but still, it’s a big light bulb. It might be a while before you see one of these popping up in a hacker’s garage…err, hangar…somewhere.

The result? One can image, in 3-D and “non-destructively” (e.g., without having to delayer or etch away dielectrics), chips down to a resolution of 14.6nm.

![](https://bunniefoo.com/ntw/srlatch-xray-ptychography.jpg)

![](https://bunniefoo.com/ntw/layout-xray-ptychography.jpg)

That’s a pretty neat trick if you’re trying to reverse engineer modern silicon.

You can read the full article at [Nature](http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v543/n7645/full/nature21698.html) (“High Resolution non-destructive three-dimensional imaging of integrated circuits” by Mirko Holler *et al*). I’m a paying subscriber to Nature so I’m supposed to have access to the article, but at the moment, [their paywall is throwing a null pointer exception](http://bunniefoo.com/ntw/np_nature.png). Once the paywall is fixed you can buy a copy of the article to read, but in the meantime, [SciHub](https://sci-hub.io/10.1038/nature21698) seems more reliable.
